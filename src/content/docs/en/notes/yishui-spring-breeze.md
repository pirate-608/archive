> **What is freedom?**

### The Sea and the Distance

As a child, I was deeply drawn to Jack Sparrow in *Pirates of the Caribbean*: a man standing before the mast, opening his arms to the sea and the distance.

![captain-jack](/images/captain-Jack.webp)

With the compass of fate in his hand, he could go wherever he wished. Perhaps that was freedom.

---

### Rules

Later, I understood that the world has rules, and life is full of invisible rules too: laws and religions, social manners, the tone of a farewell or an apology, even the posture of walking.

---

### A Free Heart

Later still, I watched an old film, *Braveheart*. The rough sound of Scottish bagpipes and the tenderness of Irish pipes moved me, but what truly shook me was William Wallace’s cry for freedom before his death.

![braveheart](/images/braveheart.jpeg)

*Everyone dies, but not everyone really lives.*

“Freedom” is a word that rises from the chest. At that moment, I felt freedom could exceed rules. With a fierce enough heart for freedom, no decayed rule could bind it.

```text
Listen, my child.
There are many things worth fighting for in your life,
but freedom is surely the most important.
Never live as a slave with shackles on your feet.
```

---

### Things Outside the Heart

A free heart, an unbound heart. But reality is that we cannot change many of the rules around us. We spend our lives learning how to live with them: studying, socializing, meeting family expectations, contributing to society, and tying personal fate to the fate of a country or a time.

Bondage does not only come from outside. It also comes from ourselves. People have desires. We want to do better than others, set goals and plans for ourselves, and walk along the roads we have drawn in advance.

“There is nothing outside the heart”? I do not agree. Objective rules are objective. They exist and affect me. Besides, are not many beautiful things, such as family, love, and friendship, also built on them? We do not want to cast these away, because they are also part of being human: not only this heart, but also the sum of all social relations.

---

**So what is freedom, after all?**

I think it may be this: do not willingly put handcuffs on yourself.

---

### University

For many of us, undergraduate life may be the closest we come to freedom. There are attachments but not yet heavy burdens, worries but not yet paralyzing fear. Most of us live on an allowance. We do not yet have to be entangled in work, family, social pressure, or the winner-takes-all rules of the outside world. University is not a utopia, of course, but it is a relatively pure place.

![sunset](/images/school_sunset.png)

You can do what you want: cultivate a hobby, exercise, fall in love, stay home playing games, watch shows, or simply sit quietly by yourself. If one spends this period locking oneself inside a cage of meritocracy, I personally feel it is a loss.

Since childhood, so many people have told us to pursue this meaning or that meaning, to work hard, to have ideals, to merge those ideals with nation and era. We are tired of it, though we have been shaped by it more than we know. As the popular line online says: “We have swallowed too much meaning; life only needs breathing.” It sounds nihilistic, but it is really a refusal to be fed more redundant “meaning.” **We may not be able to change the grand narratives outside us, but we can refuse to put their handcuffs on ourselves.**

---

### Yishui Spring Breeze

Imagine this: in middle age, you have published no *Science* or *Nature* papers and won no grand teaching prize, but at least you do not have insomnia, diabetes, or high blood pressure. You wake naturally at nine, arrive at work at ten, read, drink tea, idle a little in an office at Zijingang, and look out at Qizhen Lake. At three or four in the afternoon, you ride home slowly through the dusk, watching bright young students pass by and remembering your own youth. Crossing Yangming Bridge, you look at the evening glow, hum a tune, and wonder what dinner your spouse has made and what school story your child will tell at the table.

I would say: this is freedom.

> **So what is freedom?**

More than two thousand five hundred years ago, Zeng Xi said:

```text
In late spring, when the spring clothes are ready,
five or six adults and six or seven boys
bathe in the Yi River, enjoy the breeze at the Rain Altar,
sing, and return.
```

![yishui-spring](/images/yishui-spring-breeze.png)

Confucius said: “I am with Dian.”

If I were to say it, this is to be with all things, neither resisting nor remaking them, and yet not being bound by them. This is the freedom I now seek. That is why I named this archive “Rain Altar,” borrowing an old phrase for my own use.
